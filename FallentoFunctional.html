Fallen to Functional

Community Spaces from Campus Wood Waste.

Overview

Fallen to Functional: Community Spaces from Campus Wood Waste is a design-build project at UC Berkeley that reclaims wood from trees removed for safety, age, or disease. The project fabricates four outdoor furniture pieces for underused campus areas, using material that would otherwise be discarded.

Each piece is produced at the Richmond Field Station Wood Lab and installed with on-site signage that documents the tree’s species, approximate age, origin, and reason for removal. The project tracks wood volume diverted, number and distribution of pieces, estimated embodied carbon savings, and feedback from students and staff who use the new spaces.

Goals

  • Sustainability: divert usable wood from disposal and reduce embodied carbon.
  • Connection to place: construct furniture from the campus landscape itself.
  • Community: provide informal, accessible outdoor spaces for everyday use.
  • Education: use plaques and QR codes to describe circular design and material reuse.
  • Scalability: test a framework for a potential Campus Wood Reuse Program.

1. Memorial Glade Bench Cluster

The Memorial Glade Bench Cluster is planned as a series of low benches fabricated from a single reclaimed campus tree. The pieces are oriented to frame views across the central lawn and provide informal seating for study, events, and everyday use.

The benches will be detailed to show grain, growth rings, and joinery, keeping the material origin legible while meeting accessibility and durability requirements.

2. Eucalyptus Grove Table

The Eucalyptus Grove Table is proposed as a continuous shared work surface along the edge of Strawberry Creek. The table will use salvaged eucalyptus and simple steel hardware to form a durable, repairable structure.

The installation is intended to support group work, teaching, and informal gatherings while referencing the history and management of the surrounding grove.

3. North Gate Entry Seating

North Gate Entry Seating is conceived as a set of compact seating elements framing the approach to the College of Environmental Design. Reclaimed wood from a removed campus tree will be used to create robust blocks and ledges at a heavy-traffic threshold.

The goal is to create a defined pause point where students can wait, meet, or sit briefly between city and campus, while keeping the source material traceable.

4. Dwinelle Plaza Study Table

The Dwinelle Plaza Study Table is planned as an informal study and meeting table located in a busy plaza used by multiple departments. Reclaimed campus wood will be configured as a long, shared surface with integrated seating or edge conditions.

The piece is intended to test how reclaimed material can perform in a high-use setting, with straightforward connections that allow future maintenance and repair.
