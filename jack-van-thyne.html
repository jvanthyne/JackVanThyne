I study landscape architecture at UC Berkeley and live in the Bay Area, a region defined by its natural landscapes, shifting light, and complex human interventions. Here, making and observing have become inseparable: building is a way of thinking, and design is a way of listening. Wood is central to this practice, its grain and density carrying traces of time and human touch, a medium that remembers. Metal, light, film, and sound enter as companions in this exploration, each a tool for shaping experience, each a way to render the invisible visible. The work seeks to cultivate subtle shifts — in perspective, in rhythm, in the perception of space — moments where the familiar opens to reconsideration. Every intervention carries with it an awareness of duration: its construction, its decay, its potential transformation, its afterlife. The temporal and the tactile are inseparable. Sustainability is a framework for attention, not a constraint. It is the acknowledgment that each choice — material, method, scale — resonates within larger systems of people, place, and ecology. In every project, design becomes an ethical and aesthetic practice: a dialogue between presence and memory, between making and thinking, between care and possibility. It is through this interplay that objects, spaces, and experiences emerge — as vessels for reflection, engagement, and quiet revelation.
