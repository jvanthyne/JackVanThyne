I study landscape architecture at UC Berkeley and approach making as a way of thinking. Building is a form of listening, and design is a way to engage with the relationships between material, space, and perception. Wood is central to this practice, its grain and density carrying traces of time and human touch, a medium that remembers. Light, film, and sound enter as companions in this exploration, each offering a way to shape experience and reveal the unseen. The work seeks to cultivate subtle shifts — in perspective, in rhythm, in the perception of space — moments where the familiar opens to reconsideration. Every intervention carries an awareness of duration: its construction, its decay, its potential transformation, its afterlife. The temporal and the tactile are inseparable. Sustainability is a framework for attention, not a constraint. Each choice — material, method, scale — resonates within larger systems of people, place, and ecology. In this practice, design becomes an ethical and aesthetic inquiry: a dialogue between presence and memory, between making and thinking, between care and possibility. Through this interplay, objects, spaces, and experiences emerge as vessels for reflection, engagement, and quiet revelation.
